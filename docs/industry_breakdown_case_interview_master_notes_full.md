# Industry Breakdown – Case Interview Master Notes (11 Industries)

This document consolidates **all 11 industries** covered in this chat, preserving the full content (including explanations, “why this matters,” interview signals, shortcuts, trends, risks, and case angles) in a structured Markdown format.

---

## 1) Airlines & Transportation — Deep Explanation

### Key Players (what role each plays economically)

#### Airlines (by business model)

**Network / legacy carriers**  
Operate hub-and-spoke networks. Their advantage is **connectivity**: they can fill planes by combining multiple origin–destination flows. Downsides: higher fixed costs, complex operations, unionized labor, and weaker cost flexibility.

**Low-cost / ultra-low-cost carriers (LCC / ULCC)**  
Win on **cost per seat mile**. Single aircraft type, fast turnarounds, minimal service. Profitability depends on volume and ancillaries, not premium pricing.

**Regional airlines**  
Feed passengers into hubs for major carriers. Low margins, labor shortages are existential. Often dependent on capacity purchase agreements (CPAs).

**Cargo players**  
Cargo integrators (e.g., express logistics) run time-critical networks; passenger airlines rely on “belly cargo” as a margin enhancer. Cargo demand is cyclical and tied to global trade.

**Why this matters:**  
Different airline types respond very differently to fuel prices, demand shocks, and regulation. Interviewers expect you to segment before analyzing.

#### Aircraft OEMs & Engine OEMs
- Aircraft and engines are **long-cycle, capital-intensive bottlenecks**.
- Delivery delays constrain airline growth regardless of demand.
- Engine maintenance issues can ground planes and destroy capacity.

**Why this matters:**  
Industry profitability is often supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. That’s counterintuitive and important.

#### Aircraft Lessors
- Own a large share of global fleets.
- Airlines lease instead of owning to preserve balance-sheet flexibility.
- Lease rates are usually USD-denominated → FX risk.

**Why this matters:**  
Leasing shifts risk but increases exposure to interest rates and currency swings. In downturns, lease obligations don’t disappear.

#### Distribution Stack (GDS, OTAs, Direct)
- Traditional GDS systems charge per booking.
- Airlines push direct sales to:
  - Reduce distribution costs
  - Control pricing and bundling
  - Sell ancillaries more effectively

**Why this matters:**  
Distribution is not just “IT” — it’s a margin lever. Cutting GDS dependence can materially improve profitability.

#### Airports & Air Navigation Service Providers
- Airports control **slots, gates, and runway access**.
- ATC capacity limits flight volumes.
- Many airports have monopolistic pricing power.

**Why this matters:**  
Even profitable routes can’t grow if infrastructure is constrained. Capacity ≠ demand.

#### Regulators
- Safety regulators (non-negotiable).
- Competition authorities (slots, mergers).
- Consumer protection (delays, refunds).

**Why this matters:**  
Regulation fixes parts of the cost structure and limits strategic freedom.

#### Loyalty Program Partners
- Airlines sell miles to banks and partners.
- For many airlines, loyalty is **one of the highest-margin businesses**.

**Why this matters:**  
Airlines are not just transportation companies; they are data and financial intermediaries.

---

### Key Concepts (how the economics really work)

#### Utilization Economics
- Aircraft and crews are mostly fixed costs.
- Profitability hinges on **keeping planes flying**.
- One grounded plane destroys margin fast.

**Interview signal:**  
Always talk about load factor and utilization before pricing.

#### Revenue Management
- Prices change constantly to maximize total revenue, not seat prices.
- Overbooking is rational because no-shows are predictable.
- Network effects mean a flight can be “profitable” even if one leg isn’t.

#### Ancillary Revenue
- Bags, seats, priority boarding, food, Wi-Fi.
- Especially critical for LCCs.
- High margin, low incremental cost.

**Interview signal:**  
Ancillaries are structural, not optional.

#### FX and USD Cost Base
- Fuel, leases, maintenance in USD.
- Revenue often in local currency.

**Why this matters:**  
A strengthening USD can wipe out margins even with stable demand.

---

### Revenue & Cost Drivers (what actually moves profits)

#### Cost Drivers
- Fuel: largest variable cost; volatility matters more than absolute level.
- Labor: pilots are scarce; unions reduce flexibility.
- Maintenance: aging fleets increase cost non-linearly.
- Airport & ATC fees: quasi-fixed, often rising.
- Distribution fees: controllable through channel shift.

#### Revenue Drivers
- Passenger yield: price per seat.
- Load factor: seats filled.
- Ancillaries: margin engine.
- Cargo: counter-cyclical buffer.
- Loyalty monetization: selling miles to partners.

**Interview shortcut:**  
Profit ≈ (Yield × Load Factor × Capacity) + Ancillaries − Fuel − Labor − Fixed Costs.

---

### Trends (structural, not cyclical)

#### Aircraft & Engine Supply Constraints
- OEM and engine issues cap capacity growth.
- Airlines keep older planes longer → higher maintenance.

**Why this matters:**  
Supply discipline can prop up industry pricing even when demand softens.

#### Distribution & Retailing Modernization
- Shift from selling “seats” to selling “offers.”
- Personalization, bundling, and dynamic pricing.

#### Sustainability Pressure
- SAF mandates, emissions pricing.
- Cost increase with limited near-term substitutes.

#### Industry Consolidation
- Fewer players → more pricing power.
- Regulators slow but rarely reverse this trend.

---

### Risks (what can break the model)

#### Fuel Price Shocks
- Hedging helps but doesn’t eliminate risk.
- Sudden spikes compress margins immediately.

#### Labor Shortages
- Pilot supply is the hardest constraint.
- Wage inflation is sticky downward.

#### Demand Shocks
- Pandemics, recessions, geopolitical events.
- Airlines are high beta to macro cycles.

#### Regulatory Risk
- Environmental rules.
- Passenger compensation laws.
- Slot reallocations.

## 2) Automotive & Industrials — Deep Explanation

### Key Players (who captures value and why)

#### OEMs (Original Equipment Manufacturers)
- Design, engineer, assemble, and market vehicles.
- Sell mostly via dealer networks (agency model is emerging but limited).
- Bear **brand risk**, **recall risk**, and **platform R&D cost**.

**Why it matters:**  
OEMs are asset-heavy with thin margins on new car sales. Their profitability depends on scale, mix, and aftersales—not just selling cars.

#### Tier 1 / 2 / 3 Suppliers
- **Tier 1:** deliver complete systems (powertrain, braking, electronics).
- **Tier 2/3:** components, materials, sub-assemblies.
- Highly dependent on OEM volumes and specs.

**Why it matters:**  
Suppliers operate on lower margins and suffer most from OEM price pressure, late design changes, and volume volatility.

#### Battery Suppliers & Charging Ecosystem (EV-critical)
- Batteries are a **huge share of EV cost**.
- Charging infra availability affects EV adoption more than vehicle specs.

**Why it matters:**  
Vertical integration vs. outsourcing battery supply is a strategic make-or-break decision for OEMs.

#### Rental, Ride-Hailing & MaaS Players
- Large fleet buyers; influence demand mix.
- Sensitive to depreciation and residual values.

**Why it matters:**  
These players stabilize volume but compress margins and affect brand perception.

#### Regulators & Public Infrastructure
- Emissions standards, safety rules, subsidies.
- Charging infrastructure rollout often state-driven.

**Why it matters:**  
Regulation can force massive CAPEX shifts (e.g., EV transition) faster than market demand would.

---

### Key Concepts (the economic logic)

#### Scale & Capacity Utilization
- Plants have **huge fixed costs**.
- Underutilized plants destroy margins.

**Interview signal:**  
Volume is often more important than per-unit margin in the short term.

#### Volume vs. Margin Trade-off
- OEMs may cut prices to keep plants running.
- Discounts protect utilization but damage brand and residual values.

#### Mix Management
- Profit varies massively by:
  - Vehicle size (SUV vs. compact)
  - Powertrain (ICE vs. EV)
  - Geography (US/China/EU)
  - Trim level

**Why it matters:**  
Selling fewer cars at a better mix can outperform growth strategies.

#### Fixed-Cost Absorption
- High utilization spreads fixed costs over more units.
- Supply chain efficiency directly impacts margin.

#### Warranty & Recall Sensitivity
- Small quality issues → massive financial exposure.
- Tail risks dominate expected profits.

**Why it matters:**  
Automotive has asymmetric downside risk.

#### Aftersales & Financing (Hidden Profit Pools)
- Parts, servicing, warranties, financing.
- High-margin, recurring, and less cyclical.

**Interview signal:**  
OEMs increasingly behave like service companies, not manufacturers.

---

### Revenue & Cost Drivers

#### Cost Drivers
- Materials: steel, aluminum, batteries (lithium, nickel), semiconductors.
- Labor: less flexible than assumed.
- Warranty & recalls: unpredictable but catastrophic.
- R&D: EV platforms, software, autonomy.
- Manufacturing overhead: plants, tooling, depreciation.
- Logistics & inventory: inventory carry is expensive.

#### Revenue Drivers
- New vehicle sales: volume × ASP × mix.
- Used vehicles: buffer during downturns.
- Aftersales: parts & service (often highest-margin, recurring).
- Financing & leasing: interest spread + residual value control.
- Software & subscriptions: OTA features, connectivity.
- Data monetization: emerging, still limited.

**Interview shortcut:**  
Automotive profit is made *after* the sale, not at the sale.

---

### Trends
- EV transition: capex heavy; uncertain elasticity; margin-dilutive short term.
- China competition: cost-advantaged EV challengers compress margins.
- Software monetization push via OTA subscriptions (consumer resistance risk).
- MaaS adoption shifting ownership assumptions.

### Risks
- Tariffs & trade policy fragment supply chains.
- Warranty/recall events (one issue can wipe years of profit).
- Commodity volatility (battery metals, energy).
- Demand cyclicality (rates, confidence).

### Typical Case Angles
- EV market entry / launch decision.
- Margin recovery without killing utilization.
- Battery build vs outsource.
- Software monetization/pricing.

## 3) Oil & Gas, Power & Utilities — Deep Explanation

### A) Oil & Gas

#### Key Players

**Majors vs. Independents**  
- Majors: integrated across upstream/midstream/downstream; diversification smooths earnings.
- Independents: upstream-heavy; higher beta to oil prices; leaner but riskier.

**Why it matters:**  
Integration reduces volatility but dilutes pure upside. In cases, clarify whether the client is price-exposed or margin-buffered.

**Oilfield Services (OFS)**  
Provide drilling/completion/equipment; paid per activity.

**Why it matters:**  
OFS are volume/capex-cycle plays. They suffer when E&P capex is cut.

**Midstream operators**  
Pipelines/storage/processing; often contract-based or regulated.

**Why it matters:**  
Midstream is closer to infrastructure—cash flow stability depends on utilization & contract structure.

**Traders**  
Arbitrage location/time/quality; thin margins, big volumes.

**NOCs & regulators**  
Control reserves; objectives mix profit and politics.

**Why it matters:**  
Geopolitics can override commercial logic.

---

#### Key Concepts

**Value chain**  
- Upstream: exploration & production (high risk, high margin)
- Midstream: process/pipelines/storage (low risk, steady)
- Downstream: refining/marketing (spread-driven)

**Interview signal:**  
Always locate the problem in the chain.

**Price taker reality**  
Global benchmarks set prices; firms influence realized price differentials, volume, hedging.

**Lifting cost**  
Extraction cost per barrel: key competitiveness metric.

**Why it matters:**  
Low lifting-cost assets survive downturns; high-cost assets get shut-in.

---

#### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Costs: capex (exploration/drilling), opex (lifting), logistics, carbon costs.
- Revenue: price × volume; refining margins (crack spreads); midstream fees; trading gains.

#### Trends
- Capital discipline; transition-driven capex reallocation.
- Localization/security of supply.

#### Risks
- Oil price shocks; political/regulatory intervention.
- Safety/environmental incidents.
- Asset stranding.

---

### B) Power & Utilities

#### Key Players

**Generators / IPPs**  
Merchant vs contracted (PPA) revenue models.

**Why it matters:**  
Risk profile is defined by price exposure.

**Transmission (TSO) & Distribution (DSO)**  
Natural monopolies; regulated returns on RAB.

**Why it matters:**  
Utilities are finance-like: regulation defines economics.

**Retailers**  
Thin margins, churn, procurement risk.

**Regulators**  
Set allowed returns, tariffs, reliability.

---

#### Key Concepts

**Marginal pricing**  
The last unit dispatched sets price; scarcity spikes matter.

**CAPEX vs OPEX**  
Renewables: high capex, low opex; thermal: fuel risk.

**Grid bottleneck**  
Generation can outpace transmission; congestion caps renewable growth.

---

#### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Costs: generation capex/fuel (thermal), grid capex/maintenance, retail procurement/bad debt.
- Revenue: generation MWh × price or PPA; networks regulated return; retail customers × margin.

#### Trends
- Electrification & decarbonization.
- Massive grid capex cycle.
- Falling renewable LCOE; rising system integration costs.

#### Risks
- Regulatory resets.
- Reliability failures/outages.
- Political backlash to price increases.

#### Typical Case Angles
- Invest in new field/plant/grid?
- Portfolio shift to renewables.
- Merchant vs contracted strategy.

## 4) Financial Services / Banking

### Key Players
- Retail banks: deposits, consumer credit, mortgages.
- Corporate banks: lending, cash management, trade finance.
- Investment banks: underwriting, M&A advisory, trading.
- Payment networks & fintechs: rails vs interface/innovation.
- Core banking vendors: infrastructure layer.
- Regulators & central banks: capital rules, liquidity, monetary policy.
- Credit bureaus/rating agencies.

### Key Concepts
- Balance-sheet business: assets generate yield; liabilities fund them.
- Net Interest Margin (NIM): loan yield − deposit cost (relative to assets).
- Deposit betas: how much deposit rates move with market rates.
- Constraint heaviness: capital, liquidity, compliance constrain growth.
- Credit cycle: growth today can be loss tomorrow.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Revenue: NIM; fee income (payments, wealth/asset mgmt, advisory, underwriting).
- Costs: labor/branches, tech infrastructure, compliance, funding costs, credit losses/provisions, fines.

### Trends
- Digitization and branch rationalization.
- Challenger banks/fintech unbundling.
- Expansion of payment options (incl. BNPL).
- Decentralization/crypto (select pockets).

### Risks
- Business cycle sensitivity and central bank dependency.
- Default risk by product/segment.
- Regulatory tightening.
- Tech debt / cyber.

### Typical Case Angles
- Improve profitability (NIM vs fees vs losses).
- Reduce branch footprint; shift to digital.
- Launch credit card/product.

## 5) Insurance

### Key Players
- Insurers: offer products, manage risk.
- Policyholders: buy coverage.
- Brokers/agents: distribution.
- Reinsurers: transfer/share tail risk.
- Regulators: solvency & consumer protection.
- Investors/asset managers: invest premiums.
- Claims providers: settle claims.

### Key Concepts
- Risk pooling & underwriting: price risk correctly.
- Premiums & claims: core P&L.
- Reserving & solvency: capital buffers for future claims.
- Reinsurance & investments: shape risk and returns.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Revenue: premiums + investment income.
- Costs: claims payouts, underwriting/acquisition, operations/admin, compliance/capital, reinsurance premiums.

### Trends
- Digital & insurtech: automation/AI.
- ESG & climate factor incorporation.
- Customer-centric, flexible products; personalization.

### Risks
- Underwriting risk: mispriced policies.
- Market/investment risk.
- Liquidity risk: cash for claims.
- Regulatory evolution.
- Catastrophe risk.

### Typical Case Angles
- Profit & growth: improve revenue or reduce losses.
- Market entry/M&A.
- Pricing and product design.
- Operations & risk management.

## 6) Healthcare (Providers & Payers)

### Key Players
- Providers: hospitals, clinics, labs.
- Payers: private insurers + public programs.
- PBMs: formulary/rebate dynamics (US-heavy).
- Suppliers: pharma, medtech, diagnostics, EMR vendors.
- Regulators: reimbursement rules, quality, budgets.

### Key Concepts
- Power dynamic: providers control delivery, limited pricing power; payers control pricing/access.
- Occupancy: bed occupancy, ALOS, readmissions are key.
- Throughput economics: ED → bed → treatment → discharge.
- Pricing & frequency: utilization management can matter as much as price.
- Revenue cycle: coding → billing → collections.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers

**Providers**  
- Revenue: patient volume × reimbursement (commercial often higher than public) × service line mix × complexity.
- Costs: labor (nurses/physicians, overtime, agency staffing), supplies/implants, facilities/equipment (24/7 fixed cost), IT/EMR + admin.

**Payers**  
- Revenue: premiums (members × rate), subsidies, investment income on float.
- Costs: medical claims, reinsurance, admin/tech.

**Core equations**  
- Provider profit = Volume × Reimbursement − Cost.
- Payer profit = Premiums − Claims − Admin.

### Trends
- Labor shortage & wage pressure.
- Cybersecurity & privacy concerns.
- Automation of claims & care management.
- AI for coding/diagnostics/throughput (slow adoption due governance).
- Shift inpatient → outpatient/home.

### Risks
- Workforce constraints.
- Reimbursement cuts.
- Supplier dependency.
- Cyber events.

### Typical Case Angles
- Improve profitability without adding beds.
- Reduce ALOS/readmissions.
- Always clarify whether client is payer or provider.

## 7) Pharma & Life Sciences

### Key Players
- Pharma/biotech manufacturers.
- Regulators: FDA (US), EMA (EU).
- HTA bodies: value assessment influencing reimbursement (EU-heavy).
- Payers/providers.
- PBMs: rebates/formularies (US-heavy).
- Distributors: wholesalers, retail & specialty pharmacies.
- CROs: run trials.
- CDMOs/CMOs: outsourced development/manufacturing.

### Key Concepts
- Patent- & access-driven business: “buy” future revenue via R&D risk; monetize in limited exclusivity window; win/lose on access more than marketing.
- Blockbuster logic: few winners fund many failures.
- Product selection: market size, reimbursement, time-to-market, success probability, safety, cannibalization.
- LOE: loss of exclusivity → generic/biosimilar erosion.
- Gross-to-net leakage: rebates/chargebacks/discounts vs list price.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Revenue: eligible patient pool → diagnosed → treated → adherence/persistence; price × volume; indication expansion; launch sequencing.
- Costs: R&D (trial costs + failures), manufacturing (biologics/sterile complex), S&M (varies), pharmacovigilance/medical affairs.

**Simplified profit logic:**  
Profit ≈ Patent life × Price × Volume − R&D.

### Trends
- Patent cliffs & generic erosion.
- Pricing/policy risk and transparency.
- Supply constraints (sterile capacity, quality failures).
- M&A/licensing to refill pipelines.
- Therapy-area focus: oncology/immunology/specialty.
- Real-world data (wearables) + analytics.
- Value-based arrangements (pay-for-performance experiments).
- Manufacturing localization/resilience.

### Risks
- Regulatory setbacks; R&D failure.
- Net price compression.
- Supply disruptions.
- LOE cliff.

### Typical Case Angles
- Product launch sequencing (countries/payers) and why.
- Market sizing from patients → treated.
- Build vs outsource manufacturing capacity.

## 8) Consumer & Retail

### Key Players
- Brands/CPG manufacturers.
- Retailers: shelf power, pricing, promotions, private label.
- Distributors/wholesalers.
- Marketplaces (e.g., Amazon): fees + ranking power; can erode brand pricing.
- Logistics carriers/3PLs.
- Digital channels: social platforms, creators/influencers (demand shaping).

### Key Concepts
- Store productivity: sales/sqft, sales/traffic, traffic per labor hour, inventory turnover, same-store sales.
- SKUs: complexity drives inventory and forecasting issues.
- Margin pressure: low-margin category except luxury; price wars/promo spirals.
- Loss leadership: strategic margin sacrifice for share.
- Returns economics: reverse logistics can wipe out margin (especially apparel/e-com).

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Revenue: traffic × conversion; basket size, price, purchase frequency; bundling/cross-sell; margin split across channels.
- Costs: COGS, labor, returns/reverse logistics, inventory holding/markdowns, distribution/last-mile, real estate, tech.

### Trends
- Omnichannel dominance: BOPIS, ship-from-store.
- Private label rise.
- Personalization via data.
- Influencer-driven seasonality and volatility.

### Risks
- Price wars and promo spirals.
- Demand volatility (confidence, inflation).
- Returns explosion (abuse + leakage).
- Inventory mis-forecasting (markdowns + cash squeeze).
- Dependency on retailers/marketplaces.

### Typical Case Angles
- Diagnose revenue decline / growth stall.
- Launch product: where and why.
- Growth strategy: new product/segment vs optimize core.
- Reduce returns.

## 9) Technology & Media

### Key Players
- Software/platform companies: distribute content or provide services.
- Cloud providers: infrastructure; AI/compute stack.
- App store/OS gatekeepers: distribution control.
- Advertisers/agencies: monetize attention.
- Right holders: music/sports/content.
- Creators/influencers/brands.
- Regulators: copyright, competition, speech.

### Key Concepts
- Scale & data economics: marginal cost low, fixed cost high.
- Network effects & switching costs: value increases with users; churn is critical.
- Freemium/attention economics: trade-off ads vs UX.
- Subscription model mechanics: retention, ARPU, expansion.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Profit (tech): users × ARPU − fixed costs − variable costs.
- Profit (media): audience × monetization − content costs − operating costs.
- Revenue: subscriptions (volume × ARPU), transaction fees/take rate, expansion revenue, ads, licensing.
- Costs: infra/cloud/CAPEX (esp AI), R&D, content/creator payouts, marketing/support, moderation/compliance.

### Trends
- Short-form video dominance.
- Creator monetization expansion.
- Cloud cost optimization.
- AI-heavy features & agentic functionality (ROI uncertainty).
- Subscription fatigue.

### Risks
- Growth saturation; rising content costs.
- Regulatory pressure.
- Cloud cost blowouts.
- AI “bubble” narrative / misallocated capex.

### Typical Case Angles
- Reduce churn.
- Price new product.
- Monetize new service/creator.
- Always clarify monetization model (freemium+ads vs subscription vs take-rate).

## 10) Private Equity

### Key Players
- PE funds (GPs): raise capital, acquire, drive value creation, exit.
- LPs: pensions, insurers, sovereign funds, endowments.
- Portfolio companies.
- Financing providers: banks + private credit.
- Advisors: legal/financial/M&A.
- Regulators: SEC/FCA/EU.

### Key Concepts
- Value-creation focus: improve operations/scale/strategy; monetize via exit.
- Portfolio logic: few outsized successes offset underperformers.
- Exit & multiple expansion: timing + operational/financial enhancements drive value at sale.

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Value drivers: EBITDA growth (ops/pricing), leverage (capital structure), exit multiple.
- Costs/constraints: financing cost, covenant risk, integration/execution cost, fees.

### Trends
- Sector focus/specialization.
- ESG & digital transformation in portfolios.
- Financing innovation: private credit, minority deals, creative structures.

### Risks
- Execution risk (ops underperformance).
- Market/exit risk (multiple compression).
- Financial/leverage risk (liquidity).
- Regulatory/ESG risk.

### Typical Case Angles
- Profit & growth: why revenues down/costs up.
- M&A attractiveness: valuation, synergies, exit path.
- Ops/pricing improvements.
- Turnaround sizing and plan.

## 11) Public Sector & Non-Profit

### Key Players
- Governments: ministries, municipalities, SOEs.
- International organizations: UN agencies, World Bank, EU bodies.
- NGOs & foundations.
- Private partners: consulting firms, investors, corporates.
- Beneficiaries: citizens, communities.
- Regulators & auditors.
- Donors & taxpayers.

### Key Concepts
- Power dynamic: funders decide priorities; executors face operational reality; beneficiaries often lack voice.
- Impact focus: do not optimize profit; define outcomes and maximize.
- Program efficiency ratio (PER) & cost-per-beneficiary.
- Political feasibility & stakeholder management as constraints.
- Funding cyclicality and uncertainty.

**Impact equation:**  
Impact = Outcomes Achieved ÷ Resources Used  
(outcomes: lives improved, services delivered, emissions reduced; resources: budget, staff, time, political capital)

### Revenue & Cost Drivers
- Funding: government budget (taxes/transfers/debt), grants, donations, membership fees, endowment returns, PPPs.
- Costs: program delivery, staff/admin, compliance/reporting/audits, infrastructure/IT, monitoring & evaluation.

### Trends
- Digital government & GovTech.
- Outcome-based funding.
- ESG-linked PPPs.
- AI & data for service delivery.
- Localization of aid.
- Increased scrutiny on admin overhead.

### Risks
- Budget cuts & volatility.
- Political shifts & policy reversals.
- Bureaucracy/corruption/misuse.
- Local capacity constraints.

### Typical Case Angles
- Funding allocation under scarcity.
- Choose initiative for highest impact.
- Implementation plan under political/operational constraints.
